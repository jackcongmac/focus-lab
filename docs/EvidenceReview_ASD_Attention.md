# Evidence Review – ASD, Attention, and Guided Digital Practice

## 1. Purpose

Understand whether FocusLab’s core approach:

"guided, structured, short digital practice"

can reasonably support children (including ASD) in:

- attention
- following instructions
- task completion

This document defines:
- what is supported by evidence
- what is uncertain
- what claims are safe
- what must be validated internally

---

## 2. Key Research Questions

### 2.1 What abilities can be trained?

Focus on:

- selective attention
- sustained attention (short duration)
- instruction following
- task persistence
- visual discrimination

Questions:
- Which of these have evidence in ASD populations?
- Which show improvement with structured practice?
- Which require therapist involvement?

---

### 2.2 What types of interventions show effect?

Investigate:

- computer-based training
- game-like cognitive tasks
- virtual/interactive environments

Key questions:
- Do digital interventions show measurable improvement?
- Are improvements short-term or long-term?
- Do they transfer to real-world behavior?

---

### 2.3 Training Dose (Critical)

We need to understand:

- typical session duration used in studies
- sessions per week
- total program length (weeks)

Questions:
- Is there any support for short sessions (3–5 min)?
- Do studies use longer sessions (10–25 min)?
- Is consistency more important than duration?

---

### 2.4 Role of Repetition & Routine

Questions:
- Does repeated structured practice improve outcomes?
- How important is daily consistency?
- What happens when training stops?

---

### 2.5 Role of Parent Involvement

Questions:
- Do interventions require parent guidance?
- Does parent engagement improve outcomes?
- Is home-based practice effective without therapist supervision?

---

### 2.6 Engagement vs Effectiveness

Important distinction:

- Children may enjoy a task
- But enjoyment ≠ improvement

Questions:
- What separates “engaging games” from “effective training”?
- What task characteristics matter:
  - difficulty progression
  - feedback
  - repetition
  - structured instruction

---

## 4. What We Can Likely Support (Hypothesis)

Based on early understanding, FocusLab may support:

- structured attention practice
- following simple instructions
- task completion behavior
- short-duration focus engagement

---

## 5. What We CANNOT Claim (Without Evidence)

Do NOT claim:

- therapy
- treatment
- ADHD improvement
- ASD symptom reduction
- clinical effectiveness

---

## 6. Safe Product Positioning

FocusLab should be described as:

"A guided practice tool for attention, instruction-following, and task completion"

NOT:

"a treatment or therapy"

---

## 7. Critical Unknowns

We must NOT assume:

- 5-minute sessions are sufficient
- specific duration guarantees improvement
- improvement generalizes to real-world behavior
- all children respond the same way

---

## 8. Internal Validation Plan (Very Important)

Because external evidence is limited, FocusLab must generate its own data.

Track:

- session duration
- completion rate
- error rate
- retries
- session frequency
- drop-off points

Parent-reported signals:
- child stays focused longer
- follows instructions better
- more willing to complete tasks

---

## 9. Product Implications

This research will directly influence:

- session duration design
- recommendation system
- parent messaging
- subscription value

---

## 10. Key Principle

Evidence defines boundaries.

Product design must stay within credible claims.

Validation must come from real usage data.
